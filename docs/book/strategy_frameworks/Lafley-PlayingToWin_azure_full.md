Strategy Is Choice

By the late 1990s, P&G recognized its need to improve in the
skin care segment, which accounted for about a quarter of the
beauty industry and offered significant profit potential. With
intense competition and evolving consumer preferences,
P&G faced challenges with its flagship skin care brand, Oil
of Olay, which was perceived as outdated and unappealing.
This situation forced P&G to make strategic choices about
how to reinvigorate the brand or pursue new avenues.

Rethinking Olay

Under the leadership of Susan Arnold and Michael
Kuremsky, P&G focused on redefining Olay to establish
itself in the beauty sector. They identified growth
opportunities among women over thirty-five who were
beginning to invest in skin care. P&G leveraged its robust
R&D and streamlined operations to innovate and deliver
superior products, leading to the launch of Olay Total Effects
in 1999, which successfully combined customer insights and
advanced ingredients to address multiple skin concerns.
The repositioning of Olay into a masstige channel allowed it

to bridge the gap between mass-market and premium brands,
gaining consumer credibility through effective advertising
and new packaging that embodied an aspirational look.

What Strategy Is (and Isn’t)

Olay’s success stemmed from a clear strategic approach
characterized by a defined set of choices that effectively
positioned the brand in the market. Strategy is not inherently
mystical; it’s about making deliberate choices that integrate
and support one another to create a sustainable competitive
advantage. Effective strategies answer five critical questions:
1. What is your winning aspiration?
2. Where will you play?
3. How will you win?
4. What capabilities must be in place?
5. What management systems are required?
These questions create a cascading relationship where
upper-level choices inform and shape the lower-tier ones.

Winning Aspirations

Winning aspirations act as a framework for defining a
company’s objectives, with Olay aiming for market

leadership and significant sales targets while creating a
valuable position in the beauty segment. For P&G, the
overarching aspiration was to provide superior quality
products that enhance consumers’ lives, leading to market
leadership and profitability.

Where to Play

Choosing where to play narrows the competitive landscape
by determining specific markets, consumers, product
categories, and channels for the brand. Olay strategically
developed a new masstige segment while focusing on key
consumer demographics, which aligned with P&G’s broader
strategic goals and inherent capabilities.

How to Win

The how-to-win choices are specialized strategies for
succeeding within the chosen domain, focusing on delivering
unique customer value. Olay’s how-to-win tactics included
innovative product formulation, a robust marketing narrative,
and effective partnerships with retailers to capture consumer
interest.

Core Capabilities

Essential core capabilities are the activities that empower a
business to execute its strategy effectively. For Olay, this
included a deep understanding of consumer needs,
continuous innovation, effective brand-building efforts,
strong market relationships, and leveraging global scale to
enhance product delivery.

Management Systems

Management systems provide the operational framework that
supports and measures strategic choices. Olay implemented
systems to foster innovation, partner with key retail players,
and track consumer engagement effectively, ensuring that
strategic objectives were met and adjusted over time.

The Power of Choices

Olay's success is attributed to its integrated strategic choices.
The alignment between brand-level strategy and corporate
decisions reinforced its competitive position. Strategy
development should be viewed as an iterative process,
embracing holistic considerations of capabilities, market

orientation, and emerging trends.

Summing Up

Effective strategy formulation involves navigating complex
interdependent decisions, making it crucial for organizations
to use structured frameworks to align aspirations, playing
fields, and capable execution. This chapter serves as the
starting point for deeper exploration into these strategic
choices in the subsequent chapters.

- Make sure to address all five strategic questions to build a

- View strategy as an iterative process, ready to revisit

- Consider the multiple levels of strategy within the
organization for a broader impact.
- Understand that no single strategy is perfect; find

Example

Key Point:Strategy as a series of integrated choices is
key to achieving competitive success.

Example:Imagine you are the leader of a new skincare
brand targeting women over thirty. To succeed, you first
define your winning aspiration: to become the go-to
choice for wrinkle reduction. Next, you pinpoint where
to play: not just in mass retail but in select online
platforms popular among your target demographic.
Then, you strategize how to win by offering a unique
formula with proven results, creating engaging content
that resonates with your audience. You ensure your team
is trained in market research, while your management
system monitors consumer feedback and sales trends,
allowing for continual adaptation. This deliberate
choice-making forms a cohesive strategy that stands out
in a crowded marketplace.

Critical Thinking

Key Point:Strategy's Nature as Choices

Critical Interpretation:One key point highlighted is that
effective strategy formulation is fundamentally rooted in
making deliberate choices. While A.G. Lafley
emphasizes this structured approach in "Playing to Win"
by illustrating Olay's strategic transformation, it is
crucial for readers to critically assess the assumption
that a clear-cut decision-making framework universally
applies across varied corporate landscapes. The notion
that strategy can't be mystical may overlook
complexities arising from unpredictable market
dynamics or institutional inertia as supported by
literature on strategic management (Mintzberg, 1994;
Rumelt, 2011). Thus, while Lafley's model provides
valuable insights, its efficacy may differ based on the
specific context of businesses and their environments.

What Is Winning

Aspirations provide the guiding purpose for an enterprise,
expressed in mission statements like those from Starbucks,
Nike, and McDonald's. While these statements are important,
they are merely starting points rather than strategies. The
critical question is: what does winning look like for the
organization? Companies should primarily focus on serving
their customers to define their winning aspirations, as this
sets the stage for strategic choices that follow.
Winning aspirations are crucial because a significant part of
industry value creation accrues to market leaders. Companies
that aim merely to participate often fail to compete
effectively. The case of General Motors and its Saturn brand
illustrates the risks associated with having modest
aspirations. While Saturn tried to participate in the small car
market, it did not invest adequately to win against
competitors like Toyota and Honda, ultimately leading to its
demise.

Playing to Play

GM’s decision to launch Saturn during tough market

conditions exemplifies a failure to aim high. Saturn's aim was
to compete defensively in the small car segment without
making the substantial investments needed for success. In
contrast, competitors who aspired to win made hard choices
and significant investments to establish themselves. GM's
approach perpetuated its corporate problems, whereas P&G
adopted a winning strategy across its operations, illustrating
the importance of aiming to win regardless of the context.

Playing to Win

Post-dot-com bubble, P&G faced decisions about
outsourcing business services (GBS) amid a challenging
economic landscape. Instead of sticking solely to
conventional options, P&G's Filippo Passerini proposed a
"best-of-breed" approach, outsourcing to multiple specialized
partners rather than one large firm, thereby creating a
synergistic value relationship. This flexible, innovative
approach led to lower costs, improved services, and higher
employee satisfaction. P&G's strategy focused on how to
win, emphasizing distinctiveness and value rather than
settling for adequate solutions.

With Those Who Matter Most

Setting aspirations requires clarity on whom the organization
seeks to win with and against. Companies often fall into
marketing myopia by mistakenly defining their business
narrowly around their products instead of focusing on
consumer needs. This approach can blind them to the larger
market dynamics and opportunities for innovation. P&G’s
aspiration in home care illustrates that winning should focus
on enhancing consumer experiences rather than solely on
product performance.

Against the Very Best

When defining competitive aspirations, organizations should
evaluate all potential competitors, not just traditional ones.
P&G encouraged teams to identify the best competitor—even
if that competitor was less recognizable—which led to
valuable insights into their own performance gaps. This
principle helped broaden perspectives on competition and
potential strategies for success.

Summing Up

Effective strategy involves making tough choices about the

business landscape, competitive positioning, and internal
capabilities. An organization's aspiration to win is crucial;
without it, the required investments for sustainable
competitive advantage will likely fall short. Winning
aspirations must translate into explicit choices regarding
where to compete and how to win, which is essential for
achieving strategic action and success in the marketplace.

Strategy as Winning

In business, leaders often hesitate to make decisive choices,
fostering a culture of mediocrity. To win, organizations must
articulate a clear definition of victory encompassing
consumer needs and competitive advantage. Strategy should
integrate clear choices about how to compete, define core
capabilities, and ensure effective management systems to
deliver consistent performance. Without these well-defined
elements, companies may find themselves struggling to
achieve their potential.

Critical Thinking

Key Point:The Necessity of Defining Winning
Aspirations

Critical Interpretation:Lafley's assertion that
organizations must establish clear winning aspirations
as a strategic foundation raises questions about the
rigidity of such a perspective. While having ambitious
goals is undeniably important, the notion that they are
singularly responsible for success may overlook other
critical factors like market dynamics and adaptability.
Companies like Kodak, which initially had lofty
aspirations in photography, failed to pivot effectively as
digital technology emerged, demonstrating that a
well-defined aspiration alone does not guarantee
success. This suggests that a more flexible strategy,
taking into account changing environments and
consumer needs, might be equally vital. Sources such as

Introduction to Product Innovation at P&G

Jeff Weedman, P&G's vice president, showcases the
innovative technology behind Glad ForceFlex trash bags,
which utilizes P&G's expertise in film manipulation from
their diaper products to create a stronger, stretchable bag
while using less plastic, providing better consumer value and
cost efficiency.

Development and Testing of New Technologies

The ForceFlex bags represented a significant advancement in
trash-bag technology, introduced alongside Impress, a
self-sealing plastic wrap. Initial test markets showed great
consumer interest in Impress, leading P&G to consider
launching nationally. However, the team remained cautious
due to past failures such as the Citrus Hill orange juice,
which struggled against established competitors.

Strategic Partnerships Over Traditional
Competition

Faced with strong competition from Clorox and SC Johnson,
P&G evaluated its options and opted for a joint venture with
Clorox rather than introducing their technology directly. This
partnership allowed P&G to maintain a stake in the Glad
business while benefiting from Clorox's industrial
capabilities.

Where to Play vs. How to Win

The chapter discusses the necessity of discerning both
"where to play" and "how to win" in business strategy. Cost
leadership involves having a lower cost structure, while
differentiation entails offering unique value to consumers. It
also highlights that true competitive advantage comes from
executing these strategies effectively.

Cost Leadership Strategies

Cost leadership allows companies to offer lower prices or
reinvest in the business. Successful examples include Mars

and Dell, which leverage operational efficiencies to capture
significant market shares.

Differentiation Strategies

Differentiation provides unique customer value allowing for
higher prices. Successful brands, such as Starbucks and
Toyota, exemplify how to create substantial differentiation
despite similar cost structures.

The Challenge of Dual Strategies

While some companies can manage both strategies, it is rare
and typically leads to companies ultimately choosing one.
P&G exemplifies this with its various successful
differentiation strategies that yield price premiums.

Strategic Variables’ Interdependencies

Neither cost leadership nor differentiation operates in a
vacuum; they require interrelated strategic choices. Effective
strategy in a company should allow for dynamic responses to
changing market conditions.

Reinforcing Choices in Strategy

Successful firms align their "where-to-play" and
"how-to-win" choices. Examples like Olay and Bounty
demonstrate the necessity for dual consideration in creating
value for consumers.

Building Strategic Capability

Companies must foster an internal strategic capability to
navigate complex competitive landscapes. P&G's approach is
to enable all functions to engage in crafting their strategies,
ensuring alignment across business units.

Conclusion on Strategic Thinking

Finding the right strategic approach can lead to significant
business opportunities and successes. The chapter ultimately
emphasizes the importance of innovation in strategy
development, enabling companies like P&G to thrive in
competitive markets.

Manage What Matters

The strategic choice cascade's last element is often
overlooked. Senior management teams may define strategy
and expect quick implementation without establishing
adequate management systems. A successful strategy
requires supporting structures, systems, and performance
measures. To ensure effective action, organizations must
create processes for strategy development, communication,
and review.

Systems for Making and Reviewing Strategy

P&G's prior strategy review process was akin to "corporate
theater," focusing on performance rather than genuine
strategy discussions. New leadership sought to transform this
into collaborative dialogues that emphasize open
conversations about strategic issues rather than defensive
presentations. The revised process limited participants and

materials, encouraging focused discussions on critical
strategic questions to foster a culture of strategic thinking and
inquiry.

New Norms for Dialogue

Transforming the discourse at P&G required a shift from
advocacy to "assertive inquiry," where individuals articulate
their views while actively seeking to understand others'
perspectives. This approach encourages dialogue and deeper
understanding, improving the quality of strategic
conversations throughout the organization.

A Framing Structure

P&G leveraged the OGSM framework for strategic
conversations, evolving it to articulate core strategies more
effectively. This tool captures objectives, goals, strategies,
and measures to ground discussions and align efforts toward
achieving strategic priorities.

Think Through Strategy

To effectively craft a powerful strategy and secure lasting
competitive advantage, companies must answer and integrate
five critical questions: winning aspiration, where to play,
how to win, what capabilities to use, and what management
systems to implement. While there are many strategic
options and tools available, choosing the right path can be
daunting. Starting with the winning aspiration is vital as it
provides a framework to evaluate subsequent choices, but it
is important to revisit and refine this aspiration as other
components of the strategy are developed.

Where to Play and How to Win

The core of strategy revolves around selecting where to
compete and the means to succeed in that arena. Employing
diverse analytical tools—such as SWOT analysis, the Boston
Consulting Group matrix, and the VRIN model—can aid this

process, but over-reliance on any single tool may lead to
confusion. A focused approach considering four dimensions
is essential:
1.
The Industry
: Understand the structure and attractiveness of different
segments.
2.
Customers
: Grasp what customers value and how it differs across
segments.
3.
Relative Position
: Analyze how capabilities measure up against competitors.
4.
Competition
: Anticipate how rivals might respond to strategic choices.

Industry Analysis

Understanding the industry landscape and identifying distinct
segments—based on factors like geography and consumer
needs—is key to determining where to play. Segmentation
plays a crucial role, and rethinking traditional maps can

uncover new opportunities.

Attractiveness

Analyzing the attractiveness of segments using frameworks
like Porter’s Five Forces assists in understanding potential
profitability. Acknowledging differences in competitive
dynamics and identifying structural advantages helps to
prioritize where to focus efforts.

Customer Value Analysis

A nuanced examination of customer value involves
understanding both distribution channel customers and end
consumers. It is critical to assess what they value and how
that translates into purchasing behavior. Engaging deeply
with customers through qualitative research can yield
valuable insights.

Analysis of Relative Position

Evaluating relative capabilities and costs in comparison to
competitors can help identify strengths to leverage or
weaknesses to address. Such assessments inform the

organization’s strategic positioning, whether aiming for
differentiation or cost leadership.

Competitive Analysis

Before committing to strategic decisions, evaluating potential
competitive reactions is essential. Foresight into how
competitors may respond informs the robustness of the
strategy, ensuring that it is viable against anticipated
challenges.

A Framework for Strategy

The strategy logic flow offers a structured approach to
navigate the complexities of strategic decision-making.
Beginning with industry analysis, moving to customer
understanding, assessing internal capabilities and costs, and
concluding with competitive assessment facilitates a
comprehensive strategy formulation process. Importantly,
collaboration among diverse teams enhances the creativity
and effectiveness of strategic outcomes.

Shorten Your Odds

In strategy, achieving perfection is unrealistic, and outcomes
cannot be guaranteed. However, defining winning,
establishing an analytical framework, and conducting
thoughtful reviews can enhance the likelihood of positive
results. Ultimately, strategy is about reducing risks rather
than achieving guaranteed success.

Generating Buy-In: The Traditional Approach

Traditional strategies typically aim to find a singular, clear
answer via extensive analysis, which can be expensive and
time-consuming. This approach often stifles creativity and
innovation and leads to compromises rather than decisive
actions. It usually concludes with an unproductive consensus
rather than strong commitment.

Asking the Right Question

Framing discussions around the question "What would have
to be true?" can redirect focus towards essential aspects,
fostering collaboration instead of contention. This approach
leads teams to more robust strategic choices as they
collectively explore possibilities rather than argue individual
perspectives.

: Clearly articulate different strategic options to confront an
issue, making the stakes tangible.
2.
Generate Strategic Possibilities
: Encourage a diverse array of imaginative strategies,
fostering a collaborative atmosphere.
3.
Specify Conditions
: Identify conditions necessary for each possibility to be
successful without critiquing their viability.
4.
Identify Barriers to Choice

: Assess conditions considered least likely to hold true,
ensuring skepticism is welcomed and addressed.
5.
Design Valid Tests
: Create compelling tests to verify the critical conditions
identified as barriers to proceeding with a possibility.
6.
Conduct Tests
: Test the most doubtful conditions first to efficiently narrow
down options.
7.
Choose
: The choice should be straightforward based on test results,
making the decision-making process smoother.

Reverse-Engineering Dos and Don’ts

Encourage diverse ideas and comprehensive exploration
while maintaining necessary focus on conditions for success.
Scepticism should be embraced; engage sceptics to validate
tests and ensure fair examination of barriers.

effectiveness within the organization.

7.Question

How should companies view strategy according to

Chapter 8?
Answer:Companies should view strategy as an ongoing

process that requires continual adaptation to changing

conditions rather than a static set of plans. Effective leaders

will continually reassess and refine their strategies to ensure

sustained competitive advantage.

8.Question

What role do external advisors play in the strategic

process?
Answer:External advisors can provide valuable perspectives

and insights that help CEOs maintain an external focus,

challenge assumptions, and encourage a disciplined approach

to strategy that might not emerge from internal discussions

alone.

9.Question

What are some common strategy traps organizations

should avoid?
Answer:Common strategy traps include: 1) The do-it-all

strategy, which lacks focus; 2) The Don Quixote strategy that

challenges strong competitors head-on; 3) The Waterloo

strategy, fighting multiple fronts at once; 4) The

something-for-everyone strategy, failing to make clear

choices; 5) The dreams-that-never-come-true strategy, which

produces lofty goals without concrete plans; 6) The

program-of-the-month strategy, which results in generic

approaches that don’t differentiate from competitors.
